# Immigration Data Analysis Project  

The purpose of this project was to demonstrate our ability to use Python for real-world data analysis by examining immigration trends in the United States, specifically focusing on approval and denial patterns over time.  

We used data to explore how immigration outcomes have changed and what those trends might suggest on a larger scale.

---

## Project Overview  

In this project, we worked as a group to build a full data analysis pipeline using Python. This included:  
- Importing and cleaning immigration datasets  
- Organizing the data into a usable format  
- Analyzing approval vs denial trends over time  
- Creating visualizations to clearly communicate findings  

Our main goal was to use data to understand patterns in immigration outcomes, not just present numbers.

---

## Dataset  

We used immigration-related datasets that included:  
- Number of approvals  
- Number of denials  
- Yearly data across multiple time periods  

This allowed us to analyze long-term trends and identify how immigration decisions have shifted over time.

---

## Tools & Libraries Used  

We used several Python libraries covered in class:  
- pandas (data cleaning & manipulation)  
- numpy (numerical operations)  
- matplotlib (visualizations)  
- seaborn (enhanced visualizations)  
- scikit-learn (basic analysis/modeling support)  

---

## Key Analysis  

Our analysis focused on:  
- Comparing approvals vs denials over time  
- Identifying trends (increasing, decreasing, or stable)  
- Examining how small yearly changes contribute to larger long-term patterns  
- Understanding whether immigration outcomes are becoming more or less favorable over time  

---

## Visualizations  

We created multiple graphs to support our findings, including:  
- Line graphs showing approval and denial trends over time  
- Bar charts comparing yearly outcomes  
- Additional visualizations to highlight shifts in patterns  

These helped make the data easier to interpret and communicate.

---

## Results & Insights  

From our analysis, we found that:  
- Approvals are generally higher than denials  
- However, both approvals and denials show shifts over time  
- When viewed across multiple years, the data suggests a gradual downward trend in approvals relative to earlier periods  
- Even small yearly differences become more significant when viewed over a longer timeframe  

---

## Why This Matters  

Although the numerical changes may seem small, they represent real people and real outcomes. Immigration processes in the U.S. are already complex and time-consuming, and even slight shifts in approval rates can affect thousands of individuals and families.  

Understanding these trends helps provide context for current immigration discussions and shows how data can reflect broader policy and societal changes.
